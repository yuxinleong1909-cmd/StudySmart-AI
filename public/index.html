Key points

  • Photosynthesis converts light energy into chemical energy stored as glucose.
  • It occurs in the chloroplasts, mainly using the pigment chlorophyll.
  • The process has two stages: light-dependent reactions and the Calvin cycle.
  • Inputs are carbon dioxide, water, and sunlight. Outputs are glucose and oxygen.
Question What are the two main stages of photosynthesis? Tap to reveal
Light-dependent reactions and the Calvin cycle (light-independent reactions).
Question Where in the cell does photosynthesis take place? Tap to reveal
In the chloroplasts, specifically across the thylakoid membranes.
Question What gas is released as a byproduct? Tap to reveal
Oxygen — released when water molecules are split during the light reactions.
Question Which pigment captures most of the light energy? Tap to reveal
Chlorophyll, which absorbs mostly red and blue light.

The Calvin cycle fixes carbon dioxide into glucose using ATP and NADPH from the light reactions.

The thylakoid membrane houses the photosystems that capture light energy.

Think of a leaf as a tiny solar-powered kitchen. Sunlight is the power source, water and carbon dioxide are the raw ingredients, and glucose is the meal the plant cooks for itself. The leftover ingredient it doesn't need, oxygen, gets released into the air — which happens to be the same air we breathe.

The "kitchen" itself is the chloroplast, and the green pigment chlorophyll is what actually catches the sunlight, the way a solar panel catches rays.
